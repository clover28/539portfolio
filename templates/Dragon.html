Mobile Game: The Dragon Scrolls

The Problem

How can we make learning Chinese characters more fun?

Learning Chinese characters is one of the biggest obstacles learners face when learning Chinese. Memorizing Chinese characters can be a daunting task that dampers learners' interest. Together with my partner, I combined my knowledge in linguistics and education with my passion in games to design a game that motivates learners.

Learning theories: Models of player motivation

Our game design is based on existing models of player motivation. Malone and Lepper (1987) and Sweetser and Wyeth (2005) proposed taxonomy of game motivators that include challenge, control, goals, feedback, immersion, and so forth. To motivate learners, we designed clear and varied goals in the game, and also provide immediate feedback. Click here to read the design paper.

Game features

I came up with the idea of breaking down Chinese characters into puzzle pieces to learn the appearance of the characters, and paired up with my partner who was interested in using sound association between Chinese and English to learn the pronunciation of Chinese. The final product is a the combination of these two learning strategies in a fantasy world based on ancient Chinese myths.

When players enter the game, they will choose an area on an interactive map. Each area consists of five levels. The smallest goal is to complete each level. The completion of a level will be indicated by the color change in the icon of this level. The second goal is to complete more areas and unlock medals of creatures in ancient Chinese myths. The overarching goal is to collect the medals to complete a broken ancient scroll. The last goal is to unlock more achievements that can be viewed in the “achievement” feature. The content of achievements are unknown until they have been unlocked. The multiple layers of goals increase the complexity and uncertainty of the challenge and increase enjoyment (Malone & Lepper, 1987).
